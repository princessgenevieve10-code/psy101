Classical Conditioning

Module 5 | Learning Through Association

Description

One concept that I found fascinating from this module is Classical Conditioning. Early in the 20th century, Russian physiologist Ivan Pavlov introduced the concept of classical conditioning. In this famous experiment, Pavlov decided that he would ring a bell before feeding his dogs. After several repetitions, the dogs began to salivate just at the sound of the bell, even when no food was present. This shows that the dogs had learned to associate the neutral stimulus (the bell) with the unconditioned stimulus (the food), resulting in a conditioned response (salivation). In simple terms, classical conditioning is learning through association. It happens when a person or an animal starts to connect a neutral stimulus with something that naturally would cause a response. Over time, the neutral stimulus alone can trigger the same response.

What I've Learned

this concept helped me better understand my habits and behavior. For example, I realized that whenever I hear the notification sound from my phone, I would stop whatever I'm doing. I realized that over time, I've associated the notification sound on my phone to be something important. Just like in Pavlov's experiment, I've associated a specific sound to an important thing that will happen. Realizing this helped me change my lifestyle. I have tried to not pay attention to my phone notification. This also made me realize that certain sounds or environments may unconsciously influence our decision, behavior, or emotions in life.

Anterograde Amnesia

Module 6 | Memory Formation

Description

One concept that I found interesting in this week’s module is Anterograde amnesia. Anterograde amnesia is a condition where a person loses the ability to form new memories after a trauma or injury is inflicted on the brain. This means that even though the person can remember events that happened before the injury or traumatic event, they cannot store any new explicit long-term memories (semantic or episodic). This happens when the hippocampus gets damaged. The hippocampus is responsible for making short-term memories into long-term memories.

What I've Learned

Learning about this condition has made me more empathetic towards people who suffer from it. It made me realize how sensitive and complex our brain truly is. This concept made me understand older people who suffer from this condition better. It changes how I interact with my grandmother, who suffers from Alzheimer’s disease. It might be a different condition compared to anterograde amnesia, but it has some similar symptoms, such as the hippocampus beginning to lose more cells as the disorder progresses. This helps me understand how they’re simply trying their best whenever they cannot interact with us properly. I learned to be patient and understanding whenever I talk to them. Learning about this condition has also helped me exercise my brain to help me in the future. It motivated me to take better care of my body and to prioritize my mental health.

Adult Attachment Style

Module 7 | Relationships & Connection

Description

One concept that I found myself heavily interested and resonated with is the adult attachment style, specifically the anxious attachment style. The fundamentals of the anxious attachment style was developed by John Bowlby. Bowlby proposed that early childhood experiences form the foundation for how a person will form relationships later in life. However, Mary Ainsworth was the one who expanded on his work by creating a laboratory procedure for measuring infant attachment styles. In adulthood, attachment anxiety is characterized by having a fear of abandonment, need for reassurance, and having difficulty with trust. This attachment style stems from an internal working model shaped in childhood. For individuals with an anxious attachment style, this internal model typically includes low avoidance. This means that they generally trust that others can be supportive. It also includes high anxiety, which reflects lower feelings of self-worth and they worry that they might not be enough to keep others close.

What I've Learned

Learning about anxious attachment helped me understand myself and other people’s reactions/actions in relationships. For example, I would always worry whenever a person would take too long to text me back, because I would assume that they are upset with me. This made me realize that I worry too much because of my need for constant reassurance. Learning about this concept made me self-reflect and better understand my emotions. I realized that I need to communicate and that having space/independence in a relationship is healthy. In addition to this, It made me understand that those people with the same attachment style as me need better regulation with their emotions. This concept has helped me recognize emotional patterns and how to feel more secure with others.

Eustress

Module 8 | Positive Stress

Description

Eustress refers to the positive form of stress that can motivate a person and improve performance. Unlike distress (the stress that people commonly know as negative), eustress occurs when a person perceives a task or challenge to be manageable and feels capable of handling it. Eustress activates the body's stress response in a way that increases a person's motivation, energy, and performance without leading to negative emotional effects. For example, feeling nervous before a test or a job interview can enhance concentration, which helps people do their best work.

What I've Learned

Learning about this topic helped me realize that stress can be good for us. I used to believe and think that all stress is bad, but this topic made me realize that stress can help us improve and grow as a person. For example, I realized that whenever I get nervous or anxious about an upcoming test or due date, I can now recognize that it is Eustress pushing me to prepare for it. Additionally, this has helped me view stress in a different way. I now understand that seeing stressful situations as an opportunity for improvement, rather than failure, has made me feel more confident when facing a situation.

Specific Phobia

Module 9/10 | Understanding Fear

Description

One concept in Module 9/10 that I found interesting and that really helped deepen my understanding of human behavior is Specific Phobia. A specific phobia is an intense, irrational fear or anxiety of a particular object or situation. Psychologists explain that specific phobias are often developed through classical conditioning, vicarious learning, and verbal transmission of information. What makes this different toward a normal fear is that the fear they experience from a particular object or situation poses little or no danger at all. However, most individuals with specific phobias are aware that their anxiety is irrational. For example, a harmless object is involved/associated with a frightening experience, a child observes a person being scared of that specific object/situation, or is told that it is scary. To further add on, after association is learned, safety-seeking behaviors, usually avoidance, become stronger and persistent through negative reinforcement. Although specific phobias can be effectively healed primarily through Cognitive Behavior Therapy (CBT). Individuals with Specific Phobias are usually recommended to go through Exposure Therapy combined with other treatments. By slowly exposing the person to their feared object/situation in a safe and controlled way, it lessens their anxiety and changes their reaction to it.

What I've Learned

Learning about this concept helped me better understand both myself and other people around me. For example, I would judge people panicking or being scared of something small and think they are overreacting. After learning about specific phobias, I learned that something I don't find scary might be a big thing for another person and can affect their way of living. Understanding specific phobias also helped me figure out why I'm so scared of needles. Before learning about specific phobias, I would get so embarrassed of being scared of needles because I aspire to be a future nurse. Anyway, by understanding specific phobias, I learned to be more patient and compassionate with others.
